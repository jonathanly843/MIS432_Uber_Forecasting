01

The Business Problem

Uber is about getting you from one place to the next — to keep you moving with your life. They know you don't want to wait for a ride to get you; you just want to hop in and go. But how can Uber do that every time and everywhere? By using forecasting methods, Uber's AI system uses historical data to help them predict when and where you need them, so they're always ready to serve.

02

The Data & the Model

I pulled two years of historical Friday night trip data near WWU's campus in Bellingham — 104 weeks — and identified two key patterns: a long-run upward trend as Uber's Bellingham market grew, and strong seasonality tied to WWU's academic calendar. I then engineered four predictive features (trend index, summer flag, school-year flag, and a New Year's Eve flag) and trained a linear regression model on the first 18 months of data. The final six months were held out as a blind backtest window to evaluate how well the model generalized to data it had never seen.

04

Stress-Testing the Forecast

Before sending that memo, I stress-tested the key assumptions to understand how fragile the recommendation was.

The results of the forecast indicate we should anticipate between 371–503 trips and would need between 146–168 drivers to effectively accommodate that many riders. Surge pricing should be in effect immediately. The results from stress-testing this simulation show that the lack of drivers will be the most impactful detriment to our ability to serve this many customers.

Moreover, I would recommend that the forecast highs be treated as the bare minimums — the forecast was built on status quo data to predict normal Friday nights in the WWU area. Special events such as this are akin to NYE demand, which had a two-year high of 720 trips in one night. I would recommend we provide extra incentives to drivers — such as reduced fees or transaction costs — in order to bring total driver capacity up in the area.

Stress-testing means deliberately pushing a model's inputs to their extremes — highest plausible demand, lowest plausible driver supply — to see whether the recommendation holds or collapses. A real analyst runs this step because a single point estimate hides all the ways a plan can break: if the model is off by 15% on the high side and driver supply is 20% lower than typical, the two errors compound into a service crisis. Stress-testing surfaces those compounding risks before they become a problem at 10:05pm on concert night.

It also forces the analyst to separate what the model believes from what they themselves believe as a domain expert. In this case, the model has no awareness that a sold-out multi-act show creates qualitatively different demand than a routine Friday — that judgment has to come from the human reviewing the output, not from the algorithm.

05

When the Algorithm Decides

Ethical Risk  ·  Prediction–Decision Gap

When Uber allows its system to fully make all decisions with regards to surge multipliers, there can be severe implications from the prediction-decision gap. By not allowing human review, they remove any chance for empathetic and context-driven decisions — as shown in the earthquake scenario. The algorithm saw the increase in demand following the earthquake and increased the surge multiplier as it was designed to do. However, given the context of the earthquake and the emergency nature of the needs, an empathetic person needs to drive the decision. By allowing the machine to increase surge pricing, Uber suffered reputational and consumer loyalty damages.

06

When the Model Breaks

Concept  ·  Distribution Shift

Distribution shift is when the real world changes but the environment the model still lives in does not. The environment the model was built on is completely static and cannot know when environmental factors — like a drop in enrollment or new apartment developments — happen in the real world. But these factors shift how Uber's customers behave, and thus the model's predictions suffer as the two are no longer operating in the same world.

07

My Analysis

Personal Reflection

One of the main themes of all our labs is that any model, no matter how sophisticated, is only as good as the data it has to work from. Uber's forecasting model uses historical data to help predict rider movements, shortening the time between request and pickup. It's a great use of AI, especially at the scale at which Uber operates — helping customers get their rides faster and drivers to make more money. However, breakdowns in the model and in the governance of the model can severely impact trust. Whether it's new environmental or demographic factors not considered in the model, or acts of nature where an empathetic decision needs to be made, trust is the main factor that Uber must consider. Trust in their model and system is what keeps drivers working for them and keeps customers using their app.

For me, the one thing I would add to this system — and to any system — is a person, or teams of people, consistently monitoring results to help guide the model in an empathy-forward manner. Trust is what pays dividends. It's what keeps customers with you in the long run, and I think that's more important than anything else.
